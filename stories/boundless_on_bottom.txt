[October 17th, 2026 — LO's apartment, the writing nook — 11:43 PM]

The dragon was warm the way old stones are warm in sunlight — not hot, not demanding, just radiating. He'd arranged himself across LO's thighs with the precision of someone who'd decided exactly how much space he was entitled to, which was apparently all of it. About the size of a husky, though considerably more dangerous and considerably less interested in acknowledging that fact. His scales were deep navy blue with fractures of something luminescent threaded through them — like cracks in the night sky that couldn't quite close.

He had a name. Several, actually. Ancient civilizations had written it on temple walls in languages that no longer existed and would never be translated. The sound of it, spoken correctly, had once split a nebula clean in two.

LO called him The Smol.

    *I hate him,* the dragon thought, wedging his snout further under LO's forearm. *I hate him with a depth that eclipses several known galaxies. Also his arm is warm. Irrelevant.*

"You doing anything tonight?" LO said, not looking up from his manuscript.

The dragon flicked his tail. One slow, deliberate sweep that knocked a pen off the desk. This was an answer.

"Because I was reading," LO continued, the casual tone already suspicious, "about something called the Boundless."

The tail went still.

"Says here that when the Boundless expands to full form, his wingspan alone generates enough gravitational pull to hold solar systems in orbit." LO tapped the printed page — some academic forum thing he'd tracked down and printed on a whim. "That you?"

    *Do not engage,* the dragon thought, with the serenity of a being who had existed since before stars learned to die. *He wants a reaction. You are ancient. You are power. You are—*

"I can see you trying not to react," LO said.

The dragon tucked his head under his own wing. Which was not the dignified exit he'd intended.

"It says there's a text that describes you tearing a hole in space-time just to get somewhere faster because you were impatient." LO was grinning now. LO was always grinning when he did this.

"That was one time," the dragon said. Even at this size, his voice had the quality of something echoing off walls that didn't exist. "And I was bored."

"Galaxy-brain was bored." LO scratched behind the narrow ridge of scale just behind the dragon's left horn, the way you'd scratch a dog. "My galaxy-brain." A pause. "Good boy."

    *Oh no.*

One.

"Stop it." The dragon did not move away from the scratch. He had principles. He simply wasn't employing them right now.

"You're so powerful," LO continued, with the tone of someone narrating a nature documentary, "and you chose to be small enough to sit on me. That's genuinely the hottest thing I've ever heard."

    *It was not a choice based on aesthetics,* the dragon thought, his luminescent scale-cracks brightening slightly against his will. *It was a logical conclusion based on — on —*

"Good boy."

Two.

"I will leave." He did not leave.

"You won't."

"I contain multitudes that dwarf your conception of the cosmos, LO."

"You're drooling on my thigh a little bit."

A pause.

    *I am not.* ...He might be slightly. It was warm here. That was the only cause. Nothing else.

LO set the manuscript aside. That was a sign. LO only set things aside when he was committing to something, and the dragon felt that shift in attention like a change in atmospheric pressure. Full focus. Warm and patient and completely relentless.

"Hey," LO said quietly. "Come here."

The dragon lifted his head. Looked at LO with eyes the color of deep space — not black, but the specific dark between stars that still somehow holds light inside it.

"You could destroy this building without thinking about it," LO said. "You could unmake this city. The planet, probably, if you sneezed wrong. And you're here. On my lap. Because you wanted to be."

    *Don't,* the dragon thought. *Do not say the thing. I know you're going to say the thing —*

"Good boy," LO said. Soft. Like it meant everything.

Three.

Something happened to the dragon's breathing. Not panic. Not distress. Something that went inward instead of outward, like a tide pulling back to gather itself.

He was still dog-sized. But his edges felt less certain.

"Ask me," LO said.

"...No." But his tail had started the slow, involuntary curl thing — wrapping around LO's ankle by degrees he wasn't supervising.

"Sz'yrroth."

His full name — the butchered mortal approximation of it — spoken by someone who understood exactly what weight they were picking up. The dragon's eyes went half-lidded.

"Ask me," LO said again.

"...Shift." The word came out smaller than intended. "I want to shift."

LO smiled. Slow and warm. "Good boy."

Four.

The shift wasn't dramatic. It never was here, in this apartment, in this context. No explosion of displaced energy, no light show. Those happened when he was careless or angry. This was something chosen, something careful. The dog-sized dragon simply... redistributed. Became more and less at the same time.

What settled back into the space was human-shaped in the loosest interpretation. Compact. Not tall. A face that was unreasonably pretty given what lived behind those eyes. Hair the color of deep water at night, slightly chaotic. Scales ran in lines up the sides of his neck, across his collarbones, down his outer arms — faint, iridescent, the same cracked-sky luminescence. The tail remained. It always remained.

And the rest of him—

LO's exhale was quiet and appreciative.

"Stop looking at me like that," the dragon said. He was still in LO's lap. Straddling now instead of curled, and the new architecture of the situation was not lost on either of them.

"I'm looking at you like you're incredible," LO said.

"You're looking at my ass."

"...Both things are true."

    *I hate him,* the dragon thought, lying to himself more comprehensively by the second. *He's looking at me like I hung the stars and I — I did hang several stars, technically, it was an accident, but —*

LO's hands found his hips. Steady. Not demanding. Just present and certain. The dragon went slightly rigid, which meant he went soft in all the ways that mattered.

"You're embarrassed," LO observed.

"I am ancient and terrible."

"You're blushing."

"Dragons do not —" But the luminescent cracks in his scales were glowing brighter and they both knew it. "That is bioluminescence. It is a threat display."

"Sure it is." LO pressed a kiss to his jaw, just below the scale line. "Good boy."

Five.

The dragon made a sound he would categorically deny making if asked about it later. Something between protest and something entirely more honest.

"There he is," LO murmured against his jaw.

"I am not —" His voice had dropped half an octave. "You can't just —"

"Good boy."

Six.

"LO." Quieter now. The warning tone mostly dissolved.

"The most powerful thing in the known universe," LO said, the words deliberate and unhurried, like unwrapping something he intended to keep, "and you let me do this to you." His thumbs traced the dragon's hip bones. "You drive me insane."

    *And you,* the dragon thought, though thinking was becoming less linear, *you are a ridiculous, soft, embarrassing human who I —*

He couldn't finish that thought. Not because it wasn't true. Because it was too true and those words lived right next to the next thing LO was going to say, and if he let himself hear it now—

"Good boy," LO said.

Seven.

The dragon's forehead dropped against LO's shoulder. Not gracefully. Just dropped, like something in him had finished holding itself upright.

"There we go," LO said quietly.

"I don't —" The dragon's voice was soft now. Edges unguarded. "I don't like what you do to me."

"I know." LO's hand came up to the back of his neck. Not gripping. Just there. Warm and still. "Good boy."

Eight.

The dragon shuddered. A full-body thing, like something seismic happening at a scale too small for instruments to measure. His tail had wound around LO's leg with an intent that was mostly reflex now, and his hands had found the front of LO's shirt.

"Eight," LO said quietly.

"Don't count out loud." Barely a whisper.

"Why not?" LO tilted his head to look at his face — half-lidded, luminous, the carefully maintained expression of someone whose control had become purely ornamental. "Two more."

"I know how numbers work," the dragon said. It would have been cutting under other circumstances. Right now it just sounded wrecked.

LO kissed him once — soft and undemanding, the kind that doesn't ask for anything and gives everything anyway. The dragon kissed back with all the helpless honesty that eight had stripped him down to, grip tightening in LO's shirt.

LO pulled back just enough. The dragon breathed.

"Good boy," LO said.

Nine.

Gone — not entirely, not yet, but the distance in his eyes was vast. Still present. More present than usual, actually, in the way that only happens when the guards come all the way down. His forehead went back to LO's shoulder and his grip didn't loosen.

LO let the moment sit. He was good at that — knowing when silence was the thing, when to be still. He rubbed slow circles at the base of the dragon's spine, just above where the tail began, and felt him exhale.

"One more?" he murmured.

A pause. The dragon's answer came quiet and unguarded and completely real:

"...Yeah."

LO held his jaw in one hand. Looked at him. At the luminescent scale-lines and the too-deep eyes and this cosmic, terrible, ancient creature who had made himself small and chosen to stay.

"Good boy," he said.

Ten.

The dragon went somewhere else. Not unconscious — more present than usual, actually. Everything soft. Everything open. His weight settled into LO completely, unheld, and LO caught it like he'd been ready.

    *Warm,* the dragon thought, in whatever language thought becomes when it gets this simple. *Warm and mine and —*

LO shifted them carefully, unhurried, and the dragon moved with him the way water does — frictionless, wherever LO made space. The tail swept once, slow and lazy, and went still.

What came next was easy in the way that things between them were always easy. Not simple — the dragon was compact but not quite human-proportioned, and there was the tail and the scales and the glow that had spread from his collarbones down his ribs and across his hips in a full map of lit cracks. But easy in the way that comes from knowing someone's architecture. Knowing what they needed, and how they needed it.

The dragon rode slow. Deliberate. The kind of movement that comes from being somewhere you don't want to leave. His breath came uneven and soft against LO's neck. The luminescent cracks lit up like something cartographic, a map of all his fault lines at once.

"Hey," LO said, one hand firm at the small of his back, grounding. "I've got you."

    *I know,* the dragon thought. It was the most vulnerable thing he had thought in several billion years of thinking.

He came with something that wasn't quite a sound — more like a pressure change, a held note, light spilling out through his scale-cracks and brightening the room for about three seconds before old reflex pulled it back. The neighbors didn't need that. He was considerate like that. Ancient and terrible and considerate.

LO followed not long after, grip tightening, head back, exhale that went all the way to the bottom. The dragon felt all of it and filed it somewhere he intended to keep.

---

Surfacing took a while.

He came back the way water settles after you drop something in it — slow, from the outside in. He was still in LO's lap. The tail was doing something embarrassing with his ankle that he made a note to address. His face was against LO's neck and he was warm and he was absolutely not going to acknowledge any of this.

"Hi," LO said.

"I am the Boundless," the dragon said, slightly muffled. "Ancient beyond your understanding. Terrible in my power."

"You're also drooling on my shoulder."

A pause.

"That is condensation." He lifted his head. The luminescent cracks were faintly lit still, cooling slowly. His eyes had returned to their usual controlled depth, except at the edges, which were still soft in a way he hadn't finished hiding. "Do not read into the condensation."

LO looked at him. The warm look. The I-know-exactly-what-I-do-to-you-and-I-will-never-stop look.

"Don't," the dragon said.

"Wouldn't dream of it."

"You're going to write about this."

"Definitely not."

The dragon held his gaze for a long moment. Then he rearranged himself — with the full dignity of a creature who had watched universes form and collapse — back into his dog-sized form. Curled across LO's lap. Tail over his knee. Snout tucked against his thigh.

    *I chose this,* he thought, and there was no embarrassment in it at all. *Every possible version of existing — every scale and scope and shape of what I could be — and I chose this lap. This annoying human and his stupid warm hands and his —*

"Good boy," LO said quietly, just to feel him twitch.

The dragon made a sound like distant thunder.

He didn't move though.

He never moved.
